ADAMAWA GUDALI

Overview

The Adamawa Gudali, as its name implies, is primarily found in the Adamawa region. According to the Nigerian National Livestock Research Survey (NNLRS) 1990, Adamawa Gudali represents 2% of the national herd.

Local Types and Historical Context

Initially, two local types of Adamawa Gudali were recognized in Nigeria:
(1) Banyo: Characterized by Rahaji blood and large horns, often with a white face and red eye patches.

(2) Yola: Had an admixture of Muturu blood (Gates, 1952). The Muturu element has been progressively diluted since the 1950s, leading to the Yola breed no longer being recognized as a distinct variety by local herders.

Physical Characteristics

The Adamawa Gudali resembles the Bunaji in conformation. It is medium to large-sized with medium-length horns and usually has a pied, white, black, red, or brown coat. Key features include thick, crescent-shaped horns, a pendulous hump, and a short head and muzzle. The pendulous hump is the most reliable feature distinguishing it from the Bunaji.

Ownership and Usage

Both Kanuri and Fulani pastoralists own Adamawa Gudali cattle. Complete herds of Adamawa Gudali are rare, and they are often mixed with Wadara, Bunaji, or Rahaji cattle. Many farmers regard Adamawa Gudali as the indigenous breed of the region. They are common in villages, favored for ploughing. When they become too large to pull a plough effectively, they are further fattened in the compound and sent to market.
