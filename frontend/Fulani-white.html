WHITE FULANI (BUNAJI)

Overview

White Fulani is the most numerous and widespread of all Nigerian cattle breeds (Blench, 1993; Meghen, MacHugh, Sauveroche, Kana, & Bradley, 1999). According to the Nigerian National Livestock Research Survey (NNLRS) 1999 and Alphonsus, Akpa, Barje, Finangwai, and Adamu (2012), the White Fulani represents 37% of the national herd. This breed is found from Lagos to Sokoto, Katsina, and Kano States, and it spreads across the Nigerian Middle Belt. However, it is significantly absent in Borno, where Rahaji and Wadara predominate, and in the south-east, where there are no resident Zebu.

Geographical Distribution

The movement of White Fulani cattle into the derived savannah and the edge of the humid zone has largely been facilitated by Bunaji pastoralists, who generally agree that the White Fulani are superior to all other breeds of Zebu in resisting diseases and thriving under a variety of conditions (Blench, 1993; Meghen et al., 1999).

Key Characteristics and Limitations

The White Fulani cattle are notable for their genetic predisposition to hardiness, heat tolerance, and adaptation to local conditions (Alphonsus et al., 2012). They have a white coat color and are fairly large, with bulls reaching about 130 cm in height and weighing around 500 kg, and cows weighing about 325 kg. They feature a large and well-developed hump, a small navel flap, medium-length horns that curve upward and are lyre-shaped. Despite their strengths, the breed has some limitations, including late sexual maturity, a long interval between calving, and short lactation length. Age at first calving is typically between 42-45 months, but it can be as high as 5 years in Fulani herds.

Economic and Agricultural Importance

The White Fulani is a triple-purpose animal: it produces milk (with a production capacity of 2,300 kg per lactation), can be fattened for beef, and is used as a draught animal, especially the bull. Crosses of White Fulani and Holstein have recorded increased milk production at NAPRI-Shika, Zaria (Alphonsus et al., 2012). The breed provides much of the beef consumed throughout Nigeria (Payne & Wilson, 1999; Alphonsus et al., 2012).
