KETEKU

Overview

The distribution and productivity of Keteku cattle have been studied in detail by Blench et al. (1998). The definition of Keteku has become more problematic in recent years due to an increasing proportion of Zebu blood in Keteku herds.

Physical Characteristics and Varieties

The Borgu Keteku, also known as Kataku, Ketari, Borgu, Borgawa, and Kaiama, is a trypano-tolerant, stabilized Muturu x Zebu cross (Gates, 1952). This breed combines Muturu and Bunaji features with white, grey, and black types predominating, and occasionally red and brown. Compared to the Muturu, the Keteku has longer horns but a smaller hump, and shorter legs than the Bunaji.

Genetic Composition & Cultural Influence

As Fulani pastoral herds push further south, they increasingly inhabit regions previously restricted to trypano-tolerant stock. This has led to more Zebu stock being incorporated into village herds. For instance, the 'Biu', a Zebu x Savannah Muturu cross found near Biu in southern Borno and described in the literature (Gates, 1952), has become submerged in the local Zebu gene pool. Thus, the name Keteku may reflect the owner's cultural background as much as the animal's genetic composition.
Population Estimates
ILCA (1979) estimated 180,000 Keteku in Nigeria.
Recent assessments suggest Keteku are significantly less common, with a population unlikely to exceed 100,000 of all types.

Geographical Distribution

In Nigeria, Keteku herds are restricted to a narrow band along the Benin Republic border in the region known as 'Borgu'. Further east, Keteku are occasionally kept adjacent to villages in Northern Yoruba land. The West African dwarf shorthorn was once common in this region, and the Keteku fills the same niche (Blench, 1993; Meghen et al., 1999).

Farmer's Practices & Government Initiatives

Farmers in the Ondo area sometimes buy Keteku as investment stock due to their combination of size and trypano-tolerance. Keteku were formerly distributed from breeding farms as part of livestock extension programs. The Government Livestock Centre in Ado-Ekiti maintains a stock of Keteku (Blench, 1993; Meghen et al., 1999).

Cross-Breeding Patterns

In contrast to other West African countries, there has been very little new crossing of Zebu and Muturu in Southern Nigeria. The lack of cross-breeding along the contact line between the two types is surprising. Among the Igbo, farmers tend to believe that the two breeds are incompatible and that cross-breeding would conflict with religious strictures. On the Jos Plateau, the continuing genetic separation likely reflects ethnic competition between livestock farmers rather than animal production considerations (Blench, 1993; Meghen et al., 1999).
