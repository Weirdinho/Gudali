N'DAMA

Overview

N’dama cattle are native to Senegambia and adjacent parts of West Africa (Starkey, 1984; Blench et al., 1998; Babayemi et al., 2014). They were first introduced to Nigeria from Guinea in 1939 on an experimental basis due to their trypano-tolerance and larger size compared to Muturu.

Physical Characteristics

N’dama cattle have a medium-sized, compact body with lyre-shaped black-tipped horns and no hump. They have a small dewlap in males and a fairly large head. Although those imported into Nigeria are generally light brown, there are black and pied animals in Guinea.

Introduction and Breeding

N’dama cattle were sold to farmers and pastoralists to improve the resistance of local herds to trypanosomiasis. In most cases, herders cross them with Zebu, resulting in few pure N’dama outside institutions. However, some pure N’dama were recorded in Northern Yoruba land.
