REPRODUCTION PERFORMANCE

Importance

Reproduction is a critical factor in determining profitability in a cow/calf enterprise. To maintain a calving interval of 365 days, a cow must re-breed within 80 to 85 days after calving. Poor reproductive performance is directly linked to the percentage of body fat in cows, as adequate nutrition is essential for the reproductive system to function properly. Developing a nutrition program is more manageable and cost-effective when all cows on a farm can be managed similarly, especially when managed in a single herd, which is common in small production units (Babayemi et al., 2014).

Impact of Calving

Calving year-round complicates maintaining adequate body condition at critical times, leading to several issues:
(1) Reduced income per cow
(2) Increased post-partum interval
(3) Weak calves at birth
(4) Low quality and quantity of colostrum
(5) Reduced milk production
(6) Increased dystocia
(7) Lower weaning weights
Poor body condition leads to a longer interval from calving to first estrus, resulting in younger, smaller calves at weaning the following year, reducing income if sold at weaning. Weak calves at birth may not get adequate colostrum, making them more susceptible to disease, reducing weaning weights, feedlot performance, and carcass traits. Cows in moderate body condition have a shorter interval from calving to first estrus than those in thin condition (Babayemi et al., 2014).

Culling Practices

According to Okoli et al. (2012), 90.90% of pastoralists in the humid rain-forest of Nigeria allow 6-10 calvings per cow within its reproductive life, while 9.10% may allow up to 11-15 calvings per cow. Most female animals culled for sale are very old, as reported in abattoir studies by Okoli et al. (2001). Cattle production and breeding efficiency on grazing rangelands of Northern Nigeria are low, especially during the dry season (Mapiye, Chimonyo, & Dzama, 2009).

Breeding Bulls

Bulls in pastoral cattle herds typically reach breeding age at 4.05 years, likely due to poor nutrition and other environmental stressors. Nutrition and feed intake of young bulls affect the age at which they reach puberty (Blezinger, 2008). Neumann (1990) suggested that bull calves around 15 months of age should not run with cows in controlled breeding.

Age and Calving Interval

Age at First Calving: In Northern Nigeria, age at first calving exceeds two years (Nqeno, Chimonyo, Mapiye, & Marufu, 2009), with an average of 4.75 years reported by Akpa et al. (2012), which aligns with data from Bunaji herds on the Jos Plateau (Synge, 1980) but higher than the 37-month reported in the National Veterinary Research Institute, Vom (Ologun, 1980). This variation is mainly due to low feed quality and quantity on communal grazing lands, particularly during the dry season (Angassa & Oba, 2007; Nqeno et al., 2009).

Steers' Slaughter Weight: Steers reach slaughter weight between 24 and 30 months of age (du Plessis & Hoffman, 2004).

Off-take Rates: Off-take rates vary between 2 and 10% per annum (Mapiye et al., 2009).

Nutritional Recommendations

Providing feed supplements could improve cattle production, but it is crucial to identify the types of nutrients limiting production in a given zone (Ndlovu, Chimonyo, & Muchenje, 2009).

Comparison of Research Institutions and Traditional Systems

Standard technical coefficients compare research institutions like the National Animal Production Research Institute, Zaria, or the National Veterinary Research Institute, Vom, with the traditional system in Nigeria (Mbap, 1996). Key metrics include:
(1) Calving rate
(2) Calving cycle
(3) Age at first calving
(4) Length of lactation
(5) Productive life
(6) Milk to butter ratio

Calving cycle

Research institutions reported:
(1) 13 months (9 months pregnancy + 3 months resting period)
(2) Age at first calving: 38 months
(3) Length of lactation: 280-300 days
(4) Productive life: 10 years (5-6 lactations + 4 years before lactation)
(5) Milk to butter ratio: 1 litre to 100 gm
In comparison, the traditional system in Nigeria shows:
(1) Calving cycle: 29 to 43 months
(2) Age at first calving: 30 to 42 months
(3) Productive life: 9 to 14 years (Mbap, 1996)
