HERD SIZE

Concept

Estimating the optimum herd size for a specific area and population requires consideration of various factors, including environmental conditions, biological capacity (performance) of the species, herd management practices, and resource use and distribution (Iro, 2009). For the pastoral Fulani of Northern Nigeria, these factors are dynamic, causing the optimum herd size to vary widely based on individual circumstances (Akpa, Alphonsus, & Abdulkareem, 2012).

Historical and Recent Findings

Historical Findings:
Cunnings (1966) identified an optimum herd size of 100-150 cattle for the Fulani.
Iro (1994) reported an optimum size of 80-100 cattle.
Recent Findings:
Adisa and Badmos (2009) reported an average cattle herd size of 41, with the majority of herdsmen (46.4%) herding 41-60 cattle.
Akpa et al. (2012) found that pastoralist herd sizes in Zaria and environs ranged from 16 to 69 cattle per herd.
Okoli, Enyinnia, Elijah, Omede, and Unamba-Opara (2012) noted that a majority of Fulani pastoralists (63.60%) maintained herd sizes of 41 to 70 cattle in the humid rainforest of Imo State, Nigeria.

Stock Composition and Gender Imbalance

An examination of stock composition in Zaria revealed a gender imbalance, with females constituting 60-75% of each herd. This is advantageous as the herd's growth rate peaks when female calves dominate (Iro, 2009). Young animals contributed about 50% of the herd size, with more females (35%) than males (15%). Breeding cows comprised 49.1% of the herd, while breeding bulls accounted for 6%.

Economic Considerations

The profitability of cattle enterprises is significantly influenced by the number of breeding cows and young females in the herd (Whitley, 2008). The large proportion of breeding cows and young females in Fulani herds reflects this. Bulls are often sold when they reach breeding age to provide income, with only a few retained for breeding purposes. Economically, one mature bull can service at least 20 cows in natural mating (Neumann, 1990).

Ratios and Proportions

The number of cows and young animals in the herd is nearly equal (0.98).
The proportion of breeding bulls to young animals is small (0.14).
The ratio of young males to females is 0.42, indicating a higher number of young females than young males (Iro, 2009).

National Cattle Population and Demand

A study by Usman and Nasiru (2005) categorized pastoralists into three groups based on herd size:
Small Scale Pastoralists (SSP): Average herd size of 17 cattle.
Medium Scale Pastoralists (MSP): Average herd size of 32 cattle.
Large Scale Pastoralists (LSP): Average herd size of 73 cattle.
The study also found that LSP had more lactating and non-lactating cows and calves than other groups, while MSP had a higher average number of steers (Mbap, 1996).
