Overview

The West African dwarf short-horn, commonly known as Muturu, is small-bodied with a blocky conformation. It has short, fine-boned limbs, a compact body, no hump, a straight back, and a broad head. The face is slightly dished, and the horns are very short.

Physical Characteristics

In South-Central Nigeria, the Muturu is generally black or black and white. The Muturu on the Jos Plateau are usually black and white but are distinctly larger than their lowland counterparts. In the Northern populations, there are more variations in color, with brown, red, or tawny animals recorded.

Distribution & Population

Muturu cattle have a disjointed distribution within Nigeria, suggesting a gradual retreat from a once more widespread population. The population is widely dispersed and often stall-fed, making them less visible than Zebu cattle. This dispersion has led to varying population estimates:
ILCA (1979) estimated 120,000 Muturu.
Ngere (1983) estimated 60,000, or 0.7% of the national herd.
Akinwumi and Ikpi (1985) reported 85,000 in five southern states.
The NNLRS (1990) estimated approximately 115,000 for 1990 (RIM, 1992).

Geographical Distribution

Isolated populations of Muturu exist along the Republic of Cameroon frontier, extending as far as South-Eastern Borno, adjoining the Michika-Mubi area of Adamawa. Small clusters can be found in the Atlantika mountains, South-East of Yola, and near Cham, east of Bauchi. Muturu are relatively common in the dry savannah of South-East Jos Plateau.
A significant nucleus of Muturu is located north of Tegina in the North-West, with diverse coat colors suggesting a link with the North-Eastern populations. Historically, Muturu were likely kept throughout Southern Nigeria, but their presence has declined recently. In the West of the River Niger, Muturu were once widespread but are now uncommon, replaced by Keteku and Zebu or abandoned by local communities.

Current concentrations

Presently, major concentrations of Muturu are in the South-East, particularly in the Cross River area and among the Tiv people in and around Makurdi. Muturu are also kept throughout Igbo areas, albeit in very low densities.
