Economic Importance of Cattle in Nigeria

Nigeria is one of the leading cattle producers in sub-Saharan Africa. In 2008, the country had over 14.73 million cattle, with 1.47 million milking cows and 13.26 million beef cattle (Tibi & Aphunu, 2010). Less than 1% of this population is managed commercially, while about 99% is managed traditionally, using indigenous methods (Mafimisebi et al., 2012). This heavy reliance on traditional management affects the commercialization of cattle products and their market prices (Abubakar & Garba, 2004).

Contribution to GDP and Livelihoods

Cattle contribute about 12.7% of Nigeria's agricultural Gross Domestic Product (GDP) (CBN, 1999). The cattle industry is a vital source of income for pastoral households and participants in the cattle value chain, particularly in the sub-humid and semi-arid regions (Adegeye, 1995; Okunmadewa, 1999; FAO, 2006). Nigerians depend on this industry for daily income through the sale, transport, processing, and marketing of livestock products such as meat, milk, skins, and draught power.

Manure and Soil Fertility

Cattle provide organic manure, which improves soil fertility and enhances water retention in the soil. Manure, containing nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, is an essential fertilizer for arable farming (FAO, 1999).

Additional Economic Benefits

Cattle provide raw materials such as bones, wool, fur, hides, and milk for the production of clothing, leather goods, and dairy products like yogurt, butter, and cheese. Oxen are used as draught animals for transportation and land cultivation, providing essential farm power for peasant farmers (Payne & Wilson, 1999; Kubkomawa et al., 2011). Draught animal power enhances crop production in areas where it is used (Kubkomawa et al., 2011; Babayemi et al., 2014).
Cattle also contribute to environmental management, as they can graze vegetation under plantations of crops such as coconut and oil palm, reducing weed control costs by as much as 40% (FAO, 1996). Cow dung is another valuable resource, used for cooking, heating, and producing biogas for energy (FAO, 1997). Additionally, cattle serve as a source of foreign exchange through the export of beef, milk, hides, and other by-products (Payne & Wilson, 1999).

Poverty Alleviation

For rural farmers, cattle offer a path out of poverty. They serve as a means of security during crop failures, and their ownership provides rural dwellers with income stability and resilience.
